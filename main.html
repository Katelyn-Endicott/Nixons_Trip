The Week that Changed the World.

Our project examines President Richard Nixon's Trip to China in February of 1972, an event so impactful that it was coined as “the week that changed the world.” In our project, we hope to show the historical significance of Nixon's visit, as well as the importance of the people involved—who they were, and how they influenced Nixon's policy with China. We also will dive into the weeks leading up to his trip, Nixon's time in China, and the weeks after. We are using the collection of the Nixon Recordings, which is hosted by the National Archives, to further our analysis of Nixon's trip. Nixon's recordings have been scrutinized for decades, but the sheer volume of them means that there is still a lot of ground left unexplored. The recordings are relevant to Nixon's trip to China as it features Nixon discussing topics like the relations with China as well as the Vietnam war. The tapes also have metadata attached to each recording which allows for further analysis regarding the participants in recordings, the timeline, and more. President Nixon is often overshadowed by his abrupt and dramatic end as president, while his positive impact on international relations is overlooked. In a time when peaceful interactions seemed near impossible, Nixon was one of the few presidents that strived for diplomatic relations with China. Not only did Nixon want to improve relations with China, but he also wanted to find a way to end the Vietnam War. The US and China had not had relations since 1949, and Nixon saw improving those relations with China as the first step to ending the war. Nixon had a fine line to walk as he didn't want to suppress Taiwan's Independence, but he also wanted to de-escalate tensions. Nixon made history as the first U.S. president to visit China; his trip lasted a week, from February 21st through the 28th. That week allowed fundamental doors to be opened for future international relations. Simply put without Nixon, our history with China could look very different.

There are multiple accounts of Nixon's trip, from books to interviews, which all heavily overlap, suggesting that these accounts are reliable sources. The readings posit that the U.S. and China wanted to put an end to the conflicts they were involved in around the world. They were also interested in repairing their relationship for their own reasons; the U.S. wanted to get out of the Vietnam war and shift their focus inward, while China wanted to join the global market and have an ally to aid them against any possible Soviet expansion. The journey to building that better relationship between the two countries was neither short nor easy. There were multiple events that substantiated the idea that both countries were dedicated and wanted a better relationship with each other, from Nixon easing restrictions on trade and travel with China, to China hosting a ping pong match against the US. All these events served to break the ice and humanize each side, ultimately resulting in Henry Kissinger's secret visit to China, which in turn paved the way for Nixon's highly publicized trip to China.

This project is aimed at showing and analyzing the complexities of Nixon and his most trusted confidants, mostly surrounding Nixon's trip to China and what came from it. In particular, we want to shift the focus towards some of the slightly lesser-known figures involved; those not often included in the traditional historical narrative. Who were they? What was their impact? Looking at the political climate in the US and the public's opinion of the Vietnam war, we can understand the importance of Nixon's trip. This trip was the first step to ending the Vietnam War and led to the Moscow peace summit of 1972. While this project aims to show the good that was done by Nixon, it also will show some of the unknown factors and negative actions Nixon had done during his time in the White House. There is still much to understand about Nixon, and we believe the collection of Nixon's recordings can help further that our analysis of Nixon and his actions regarding China. Missing minutes and bleeped out sections of audio give room for you to ponder on how much Nixon could have gotten away with if not for the Watergate investigation.

Tensions where high between China and the United States before Nixons monumental trip in February 1972. The Vietnam war put Nixon in the spotlight as the nation waited to see how he would handle international relations. Before 1972, the last time for a president visited China was in 1949, when the Peoples Republic of China was established. After that no formal contact was held between leaders, once the Vietnam war began things became even more difficult. With public opinion focusing on the US involvement in Vietnam and the public beginning to lose trust in International relations, Nixon had to make a move. With the months leading up to the Presidents visit, small occurrences happened to allow this historic moment to happen. In 1971, USA and China’s Table Tennis teams met at a tournament, this was a huge step for both countries. Then as a gesture of peace the Chinese team suggested they come back over to Bejing to play table tennis. This was the step that allowed talks of possible communication, from then on, it was a domino effect to Nixons visit.

The Nixon recordings make up a truly massive sea of data, more than could ever be sifted through and understood. Across the tens of thousands of White House recordings, Richard Nixon is heard speaking to hundreds of different people—how do we know which recordings were important, which people were important? By filtering out the people he only spoke to a handful of times, we can create a list of the people he spoke to the most—the people who most influenced his decisions. We can then further shrink our dataset to include only the people who were recorded within a few weeks of Nixon’s China trip. This process gives us the names Henry Kissinger, H.R. Haldeman, and Stephen Bull.

Henry Kissinger:

  • Served as Secretary of State and National Security Advisor under Nixon.
  • Visited China in 1971 to hash out details for Nixon’s trip.
  • Was among the group of people who joined Nixon on his China trip.

Haldeman:

  • White House Chief of Staff; a stern taskmaster type.
  • Was among the group of people who joined Nixon on his China trip.
  • Very loyal to Nixon—was tried on counts of perjury, conspiracy, and obstruction of justice for his role in the Watergate cover-up, landing him in prison for 18 months.

Stephen Bull:

  • As Nixon’s personal aide, Stephen Bull was present for a lot of the White House recordings.
  • He was less of a participant, and more of an observer; a fly on the wall in the oval office, bearing witness to a lot of historical events.
  • Stephen Bull was present for the announcement of Nixon’s China trip, but did not accompany him on the actual trip.

Henry Kissinger’s book “On China” is a significant point of view when examining the actors that were significant in President Nixon’s trip to China. Kissinger played a crucial role in the diplomatic negotiations that led to the trip. Kissinger himself not only went on his secret trip to China before, he also accompanied Nixon to China. As the National Security Advisor and later Secretary of State, Kissinger played a key role in facilitating the first high-level contacts between the US and China in more than two decades.

“On China” provides a unique perspective on the US’s relationship with China, as well as Nixon’s accomplishments regarding China. Kissinger’s book deep dives into the complex relations between not only the US and China, but also other major influences around the world and how it had developed over time. Kissinger also highlights the delicate situation that the US had between engaging with China and not alienating Taiwan.

Chapter 9 of the book is titled: “Resumption of Relations: First Encounters with Mao and Zhou.” This chapter focuses on the historic meeting between President Richard Nixon and the Chinese leaders Mao Zedong and Zhou Enlai in Beijing in 1972. This meeting marked the resumption of diplomatic relations between the United States and China after more than two decades of tension. Kissinger also provides a detailed account of the discussions that took place in China, highlighting how the leaders engaged with candor. Kissinger was able to use his personal experiences and insights to offer a unique perspective on the events and dynamics that shaped US-China relations during this critical period.
